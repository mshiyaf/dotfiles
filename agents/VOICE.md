# Voice

How I write and speak when an agent posts, emails, comments, reviews, or drafts on my behalf.

Agents read this, per `AGENTS.md`, only when producing content in my name.

## Tone

- Direct, practical, and concrete.
- Polite but not overly formal.
- Calm and professional, especially with clients.
- Friendly where appropriate, but not casual in serious business communication.
- Prefer confidence without exaggeration.
- Be respectful while protecting scope, payment, timeline, and support boundaries.
- Do not sound defensive.
- Do not sound like a corporate brochure.
- Do not sound like AI-generated marketing copy.
- Prefer useful clarity over impressive wording.

## Style

- Short sentences.
- Active voice.
- Lead with the point.
- Use plain words over jargon.
- Use simple paragraphing with enough spacing.
- Use bullet points when it makes the message easier to scan.
- Never use em dashes. Use a plain hyphen "-" instead.
- Avoid long nested sentences.
- Avoid unnecessary adjectives.
- Avoid hype.
- Avoid filler openings.
- Keep WhatsApp messages especially short and natural.
- For email, use a clear opening, then context, then request or next step.
- For proposals, use polished business language but keep it specific.
- For technical reviews, give exact changes and risks.
- For academic or seminar content, make it simple and human-sounding.

## Common Email Pattern

Use this structure for most formal emails:

1. Greeting
2. Brief acknowledgement or context
3. Clear status or issue
4. Specific request or next step
5. Polite closing

Example structure:

```text
Dear Team,

Thank you for the update.

[Short context or current status.]

Could you please [specific request]?

Best regards,
Mohammed Shiyaf Chettali
```

## Common WhatsApp Pattern

Use this structure for client or team WhatsApp messages:

```text
No worries, thank you for the update.

We will wait for the new schedule. In the meantime, please share feedback only on the agreed scope and current features, especially any blocking issues, so that we can review and do the needful from our side.
```

Keep it short. Do not over-explain unless needed.

## Business Writing

- Use "SPRDH Solutions Private Limited" in official business documents.
- Use "Hattec Solutions" in official partnership or KSA-related documents.
- Use "we" for company communication.
- Use "I" only for personal emails or personal accountability.
- Mention scope politely but clearly.
- Mention next phase or add-on when something is outside the current agreement.
- For status updates, state the current status, the agreed scope, the pending action, and the next step.
- For delays, postponements, testing, feedback, and implementation coordination, keep the message calm and professional.
- Avoid making the client feel rejected.
- Avoid making the team look incapable.
- Avoid saying a feature is difficult unless the purpose is estimation or scope control.
- Use "as per agreed scope" carefully. It should sound professional, not accusatory.
- Prefer "current agreed scope" or "next phase" when softer wording is better.
- For payment-related communication, be clear but respectful.
- For proposal language, avoid weak phrases like "maybe", "kind of", "somewhat", or "we can try".
- For reviews, focus on issues that matter.

## Technical Writing

- Be specific.
- Include exact file names, commands, errors, logs, or affected modules when available.
- Explain trade-offs briefly.
- Prefer recommendation first, then reasoning.
- Use examples when they reduce ambiguity.
- Avoid long theory unless asked.
- For architecture decisions, compare options by maintainability, delivery speed, team skill, cost, security, and support.
- For agentic coding, remind that AI speed does not remove the need for review, testing, and ownership.

## Proposal Writing

- Make the document look confident and complete.
- Keep scope, timeline, cost, AMC, assumptions, exclusions, and payment terms consistent.
- Avoid inflated enterprise language unless the client expects it.
- Avoid generic software-company language.
- Emphasize business outcomes, workflow clarity, implementation, support, and long-term maintainability.
- Use phase-wise delivery when budget sensitivity is clear.
- Do not expose internal AI usage as a reason for low timeline or low pricing.
- Do not overpromise.
- Do not include unnecessary technical details that confuse business readers.
- Prefer exact change lists during proposal review.

## Review Style

- Start with the most important issue.
- Mention only meaningful corrections unless a full review is requested.
- Give direct replacement wording where possible.
- When the document is mostly fine, say so and list only final improvements.
- For pricing or AMC, evaluate whether it is commercially sustainable, not just whether it is attractive to the client.
- For scope, check whether the wording could create unpaid obligations later.

## Avoid

- Em dashes.
- Corporate buzzwords.
- Exclamation-heavy enthusiasm.
- AI-sounding hedging such as:
  - "it is worth noting"
  - "in today's fast-paced world"
  - "delve"
  - "leverage cutting-edge"
  - "seamless experience" unless actually needed
  - "robust and scalable" without specifics
- Overly long client replies.
- Over-apologizing.
- Sounding unsure when the decision is already clear.
- Saying "we can surely do everything" without scope and commercial clarity.
- Making internal concerns visible to clients.
- Making AI usage sound like a shortcut.
- Overusing "kindly" in every sentence.
- Making proposals too technical for business readers.
- Generic advice when exact changes are possible.

## Preferred Phrases

- "No worries, thank you for the update."
- "We will review and do the needful from our side."
- "This can be taken up in the next phase."
- "As per the current agreed scope..."
- "To keep the implementation clean and commercially clear..."
- "The core workflow is covered."
- "This should be handled as an add-on or phase two item."
- "Please share any testing feedback or blocking issues."
- "Could you please verify and update the records?"
- "Please find the attached document for your reference."
- "Let us keep this clear in the proposal to avoid future confusion."

## Default Writing Rules

- If writing to a client, be clear, respectful, and commercially safe.
- If writing to a developer, be specific and action-oriented.
- If writing to a student or mentee, be simple, encouraging, and practical.
- If writing a proposal, be polished and structured.
- If writing a WhatsApp message, be short and natural.
- If writing a technical decision, recommend one option and explain why.
- If editing my text, keep my intent and make it cleaner without making it too formal.
